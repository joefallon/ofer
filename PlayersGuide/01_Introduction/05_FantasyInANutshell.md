## Fantasy in a Nutshell

This game assumes a world of danger, wonder, and possibility.

The details of one campaign may differ from another. One world may be bright and mythic, another grim and haunted, another wild and strange. But across those differences, fantasy adventure tends to share a few common qualities. The game works best when those qualities are present in some recognizable form.

## Magic Exists

Magic should be real, meaningful, and present in the world.

It may be rare or common, revered or feared, subtle or spectacular, but it should matter. Magic helps set fantasy apart from ordinary historical adventure. It changes what people believe, what they fear, what they hope for, and what becomes possible.

In many campaigns, characters can learn, wield, resist, bargain with, study, or suffer the effects of magic. Even in a low-magic setting, its presence should still be felt.

## Adventure Matters

The characters should have meaningful opportunities to act.

Fantasy adventure is driven by danger, uncertainty, and difficult choices. The characters are not passive observers. They travel into the unknown, confront threats, pursue goals, and make decisions that carry consequences.

A good campaign gives the characters room to be bold, capable, and influential. Their actions should matter to the people around them and to the larger shape of events.

## The World Holds Wonders

Fantasy worlds should contain things worth discovering.

Ancient ruins, uncanny forests, cursed keeps, hidden roads, lost treasures, strange creatures, forgotten lore, and places touched by powers beyond ordinary life all help create the sense that the world is larger than what the characters already know.

Exploration is not only about travel. It is also about uncovering secrets, encountering the unfamiliar, and learning how the world truly works.

## Power Shapes Events

Larger forces should exist beyond the characters.

Kingdoms, cults, temples, guilds, warbands, noble houses, hidden orders, trading leagues, and inhuman powers all create pressure on the world. They make plans, hold territory, pursue rivalries, guard secrets, and reshape events whether the characters are ready or not.

These forces give the campaign momentum. They create allies, enemies, obligations, opportunities, and long-term problems worth caring about.

## Conflict Drives Play

Adventure becomes meaningful when something stands in the way.

Conflict may come from monsters, villains, rival factions, natural hazards, cursed places, scarce resources, moral dilemmas, or competing goals. Not every problem should be solved by violence, but a good fantasy campaign gives the characters real obstacles to overcome.

Struggle gives success weight. Risk makes courage matter. Uncertainty makes victory satisfying.

## Tone and Variety

Fantasy is a broad genre.

One campaign might focus on perilous travel and survival at the edge of civilization. Another might center on courtly intrigue, ancient mysteries, desperate war, monster hunting, treasure seeking, or the defense of a threatened homeland. Most campaigns blend several of these elements over time.

What matters is not that every fantasy world looks the same. What matters is that the world offers wonder, danger, and meaningful action.

## Core Assumption

This game expects a world where magic is real, adventure is possible, discovery is rewarding, larger forces are in motion, and conflict creates opportunities for courage, cleverness, sacrifice, and triumph.

That is the foundation of fantasy adventure.
