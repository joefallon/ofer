## Other Tools You Need

You do not need much to start playing.

At a minimum, each player should have:

- **a character sheet**
- **a way to write and erase changes**
- **a set of dice, or access to shared dice**
- **a place to keep notes**

That is enough to begin.

A roleplaying game does not require an elaborate setup. You do not need miniatures, maps, custom terrain, or expensive accessories to have a good session. Those things can be helpful, but they are optional.

## Basic Supplies

The most useful tools at the table are simple ones.

### Character Sheet

Your character sheet is your main play aid.

It keeps track of the information you need during play, such as:

- **your character's abilities**
- **equipment**
- **resources**
- **important traits**
- **damage, healing, and other changing values**

Keep it somewhere easy to read and update.

### Pencil or Pen

You will need something to record changes during play.

A pencil is often the easiest option because character information changes frequently. Hit points, equipment, money, spell use, ammunition, rations, and notes may all shift over the course of an adventure.

If you use a pen, it helps to keep extra paper nearby.

### Eraser

If you use pencil, an eraser is worth having close at hand.

Characters change often, especially early in play. A good eraser makes those adjustments easier to manage and keeps the sheet readable.

### Dice

Most groups use physical polyhedral dice, though digital rollers work too.

It is helpful to have access to:

- **d4**
- **d6**
- **d8**
- **d10**
- **d12**
- **d20**

You do not need to own multiple sets when starting out. Sharing dice is fine.

### Notes

A small notebook, scratch paper, or note-taking app can be surprisingly valuable.

Use it to track things like:

- **names of important people**
- **places you want to revisit**
- **clues**
- **debts, promises, and rumors**
- **party plans**
- **questions to ask later**

Good notes save time and help the world feel more connected from session to session.

## Useful Optional Tools

The following are helpful, but not required:

- **index cards for quick reference**
- **tokens or coins for tracking temporary resources**
- **a folder or binder for character papers**
- **a dice tray, if the table is crowded**
- **shared party notes**
- **digital copies of your character materials**
- **reference pages for commonly used rules**

These tools can make play smoother, but none of them are necessary for a successful game.

## Playing In Person Or Online

The exact tools may change depending on how the group plays.

For an in-person game, players often use:

- **printed sheets**
- **physical dice**
- **paper notes**
- **books or printouts**

For an online game, players often use:

- **digital character sheets**
- **virtual dice rollers**
- **shared documents**
- **voice or video chat**
- **online maps or handouts**

Either approach works. The important thing is that everyone can easily access the information they need during play.

## Keep It Simple

New players sometimes assume they need a full collection of accessories before they can begin.

They do not.

A character sheet, dice, and a way to take notes are enough for a real game. Everything else is a convenience, not a requirement.

Start with the basics. Add more tools only when they solve a real problem for your group.

## Core Idea

The best table tools are the ones that help players:

- **understand the current situation**
- **track their characters clearly**
- **make decisions easily**
- **stay engaged in play**

If a tool helps with that, it is useful.

If it gets in the way, you can do without it.
