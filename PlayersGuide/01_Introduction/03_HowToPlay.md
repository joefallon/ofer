## How to Play

This game is played through conversation, imagination, choice, and rules.

The game master describes the world, the situation, and what is happening around the characters. The players describe what their characters say, do, attempt, and avoid. When the outcome of an action is obvious, the group simply continues. When the outcome is uncertain and the result matters, the rules and dice help decide what happens next.

Play continues as a back-and-forth exchange:

- **the game master presents the situation**
- **the players decide what their characters do**
- **the world responds**
- **the situation changes**
- **play moves forward**

That rhythm is the heart of the game.

## Collaborative Play

This is a shared game, not a contest between the players and the game master.

The goal is not to "win" in the board-game sense. The goal is to take part in an exciting adventure, make meaningful choices, face uncertain outcomes, and discover what happens together. Success can be thrilling, but setbacks, mistakes, surprises, and narrow escapes are part of the fun too.

The game master introduces places, characters, opportunities, dangers, and complications. The players respond by choosing what their characters care about and what they attempt. Those choices change the direction of play. The game master then reacts to those choices by presenting new developments, new information, and new consequences.

This back-and-forth continues throughout the session.

## What Play Looks Like

A typical moment of play often works like this:

1. **The game master describes the current situation.**
2. **The players ask questions if they need more clarity.**
3. **The players decide what their characters do.**
4. **The game master describes the result.**
5. **If the outcome is uncertain, the rules and dice help resolve it.**
6. **The new situation becomes the next moment of play.**

Sometimes that cycle moves quickly. Sometimes the group slows down to plan, investigate, negotiate, or argue about a risky choice. Both are normal.

## Example of Play

The characters arrive at a roadside inn late at night during a cold rainstorm.

The game master describes the scene: a crowded common room, muddy boots by the door, a worried innkeeper, a pair of cloaked travelers watching from the corner, and a loud thump now and then from somewhere below the floorboards.

The players decide what interests their characters.

- One character questions the innkeeper.
- Another watches the cloaked strangers.
- A third asks whether the thumping sounds human.

From there, the game can move in many directions.

If the characters press the innkeeper for answers, they may learn that something dangerous is loose in the cellar. If they focus on the travelers, they may uncover a different problem entirely. If they ignore both and settle in for food and drink, the game master may introduce a new event: a scream outside, a power outage, a missing child, or the sudden crash of a cellar door bursting open.

The game master presents the world.

The players choose what matters.

The story develops from that interaction.

## The Dice

Most of the game happens through description, questions, and decisions. Dice enter the picture when something is risky, contested, or uncertain.

This game uses the following dice:

- **d4**
- **d6**
- **d8**
- **d10**
- **d12**
- **d20**

These are often called polyhedral dice.

It is helpful for each player to have one full set, but sharing dice at the table works fine too.

## What Dice Mean

Dice introduce uncertainty.

A die roll does not replace the character's abilities. Instead, it works together with them. The roll represents chance, pressure, friction, and the unpredictable nature of action under uncertain conditions. A capable character usually performs better than an incapable one, but even skilled characters can fail and even desperate attempts can succeed.

That mix of skill and uncertainty is part of what makes play exciting.

## Reading Dice Expressions

Sometimes you roll a single die. Sometimes the rules tell you to roll multiple dice and add them together.

For example:

- **1d20** means roll one twenty-sided die
- **1d8** means roll one eight-sided die
- **2d6** means roll two six-sided dice and add them together
- **2d4 + 2** means roll two four-sided dice, add them together, then add 2

The first number tells you how many dice to roll.

The number after the `d` tells you what kind of die to use.

## Percentile Dice

Sometimes you may need to roll a number from 1 to 100.

To do that, roll two ten-sided dice:

- one die represents the **tens digit**
- the other die represents the **ones digit**

If one die shows 5 and the other shows 2, the result is **52**.

If both dice show 0, the result is **100**.

## Core Mechanic: Rolling a d20

The most common procedure in the game follows a simple pattern:

1. **Roll a d20.**
2. **Apply any relevant bonuses or penalties.**
3. **Compare the total to a target number.**

If the total equals or exceeds the target number, the action succeeds.

If the total is lower than the target number, the action fails or succeeds at a cost, depending on the situation and the rules being used.

Higher results are generally better.

## When to Roll

Not every action needs a die roll.

A roll is usually appropriate when:

- **the outcome is uncertain**
- **failure would matter**
- **success is not automatic**
- **the moment carries risk, pressure, or consequence**

If there is no meaningful uncertainty, the game master can simply decide the outcome based on the fiction and move on.

## What You Need to Play

To begin play, each player usually needs:

- **access to the rules**
- **a character sheet**
- **dice**
- **something to write with or type notes into**

That is enough to start.

Other tools can help, but they are optional:

- **maps**
- **miniatures**
- **tokens**
- **scratch paper**
- **digital notes**
- **reference sheets**

These can make play easier or more vivid, but they are not required. At its core, the game runs on shared understanding, clear communication, and a willingness to engage with the world.

## Core Idea

The game master presents a situation.

The players decide what their characters do.

The rules help resolve uncertainty.

The results create the next situation.

That cycle repeats until the session ends, the danger is overcome, or the characters decide to head somewhere even more dangerous.
