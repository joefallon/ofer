## Safety Tools

A roleplaying game can include danger, fear, conflict, and other intense material. The characters may face discomfort, but the players should still feel safe, respected, and able to speak up when something is not working for them.

Safety tools are the table practices that help make that possible.

At a minimum, a group should agree on two things before play begins:

- **what kinds of content people do not want in the game**
- **what the group will do if uncomfortable content appears during play**

These tools are not obstacles to fun. They help protect it.

## Begin With a Conversation

Before the campaign starts, take time to talk openly about comfort levels, boundaries, and concerns.

A good time for this conversation is during early campaign setup, often before or during character creation. The game master can help lead the discussion, but maintaining a safe and respectful table is everyone's shared responsibility.

During this conversation, each participant should have the chance to name themes, topics, or kinds of content they do not want included in play.

These may be:

- **very specific**, such as spiders, drowning, or eye injuries
- **broader categories**, such as abuse, disease, or loss of control
- **tone concerns**, such as cruelty between party members or humiliation played for laughs

No one needs to justify a boundary. A person saying that something is unwelcome is enough.

## Privacy Matters

Not everyone is comfortable explaining personal concerns in front of a group.

Make it clear that players can also raise concerns privately with the game master if they prefer. If someone shares a concern in private, that preference should be respected without pressure for explanation.

The goal is not to make people disclose more than they want. The goal is to give them a reliable way to protect their comfort and participation.

## Lines and Veils

Many groups find it useful to sort sensitive material into two broad categories:

- **Lines** are topics that will not appear in the game.
- **Veils** are topics that may exist in the background, but will not be described in detail or played out directly.

For example:

- A group might place **sexual violence** behind a line, meaning it is excluded completely.
- A group might place **consensual intimacy** behind a veil, meaning it can be implied without being acted out at the table.
- A group might place **torture**, **harm to children**, or **body horror** behind lines or veils depending on comfort.

This approach gives the table a practical shared vocabulary for boundaries.

## Topics Worth Discussing

Every group is different, but it is often wise to ask about content such as:

- **graphic violence**
- **harm to children**
- **harm to animals**
- **sexual content**
- **assault or coercion**
- **mental health themes**
- **disease, injury, or bodily horror**
- **addiction**
- **religion or religious imagery**
- **prejudice, oppression, or discrimination**
- **self-harm or suicide**
- **phobias and common triggers specific to the group**

For darker campaigns, horror games, or especially intense subject matter, a written checklist can help people think through concerns they might not remember in the moment.

## Keep the Conversation Respectful

Boundary discussions should be taken seriously.

That means:

- **do not pressure people to explain themselves**
- **do not argue with stated boundaries**
- **do not make jokes that make others less willing to speak honestly**
- **do ask respectful clarifying questions if a boundary is unclear**
- **do treat the conversation as normal and important table setup**

A calm, matter-of-fact approach usually works best.

## Agree on What Happens During Play

The group should also decide what to do if a problem comes up in the moment.

Even with good preparation, something uncomfortable may still appear unexpectedly. When that happens, everyone should already know how to signal concern and what kinds of responses are available.

A table may choose one or more simple tools, such as:

- **a plain verbal pause**, such as "pause" or "let's stop for a second"
- **a step-away phrase**, such as "let's take five"
- **color signals**, such as red for stop, yellow for slow down, and green for okay
- **a nonverbal check-in gesture**
- **private messages in online play**

The exact method matters less than making sure everyone understands it and feels able to use it.

## When a Boundary Is Reached

If someone signals a problem, stop and check in.

Depending on the situation, the group may decide to:

- **skip the content**
- **fade to black**
- **rewind and play the scene a different way**
- **change the description**
- **take a short break**
- **end the session early**

Different moments call for different responses. The important thing is that the group responds quickly, respectfully, and without treating the concern as an inconvenience.

## Boundaries Can Change

A first conversation is not always the final word.

Sometimes people need time to think. Sometimes a topic feels different in play than it did during session preparation. Sometimes a player realizes a concern only after it appears.

Because of that, boundaries should be revisited when needed.

Players should be able to add new lines, add new veils, or raise concerns later without being made to feel that they are disrupting the game.

## Shared Responsibility

The game master helps guide the process, but safety is not the game master's burden alone.

Everyone at the table should help create an environment where people can:

- **speak honestly**
- **set boundaries without embarrassment**
- **raise concerns during play**
- **trust that those concerns will be respected**

That trust makes better play possible.

## Core Principle

The game may include danger, fear, grief, conflict, and uncertainty.

The players should still feel safe, respected, and able to protect their own limits.

A good table does not ignore discomfort and hope for the best. It creates clear ways to address problems so the group can keep playing with confidence.
