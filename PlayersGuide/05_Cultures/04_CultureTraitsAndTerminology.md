## Culture Traits and Terminology

Each culture grants a set of traits that reflect the kind of life your character lived before becoming an adventurer.

These traits are not meant to represent biology, ancestry, or inherited physical qualities. Instead, they represent things your character learned through upbringing, environment, custom, practice, and everyday experience.

A culture's traits often reflect one or more of the following:

- the community where the character was raised
- the environment they grew up in
- the skills or habits daily life required
- the customs and expectations they learned
- the languages commonly spoken around them

Culture traits should feel like learned experience. If a trait would still make sense even if the character had been born into a different lineage but raised in the same way, it is probably a good candidate for culture.

### Common Types of Culture Traits

Culture traits often fall into a few broad categories.

#### Environmental Familiarity

Some cultures teach characters how to move through a particular kind of environment. A character raised in a major city might know how to navigate crowds, find public destinations, or stay alert in busy streets. A character raised in harsh wilderness might know how to travel difficult ground, read weather, or avoid natural dangers.

#### Learned Knowledge

Some cultures grant knowledge tied to customs, history, religion, trade, craft, survival, or other parts of everyday life. This knowledge comes from exposure and experience rather than formal scholarship alone.

#### Social Habits and Practical Training

Many cultures teach ways of speaking, observing, bargaining, hosting, judging danger, finding help, or handling routine challenges within a community. These are learned behaviors and practical skills shaped by daily life.

#### Languages

Languages are often part of culture because they are commonly learned through family, community, trade, education, and social contact. A culture may grant one or more languages that reflect the world in which the character was raised.

### Culture Terms in Play

When a rule refers to your culture, it means the upbringing and way of life that most strongly shaped your character before play begins.

When a trait refers to a culture you aren't familiar with, it means a culture outside your own lived experience or regular upbringing, even if you have encountered it in passing.

When choosing or describing a culture, think in terms of community and lived experience. Culture is not a measure of worth, purity, sophistication, or moral character. It is simply a way to describe the learned background that helped shape your character.

### Culture and Character Identity

Your culture is an important part of your character, but it is not the whole character.

It does not decide your personality, beliefs, ambitions, or moral outlook. Two characters with the same culture may behave very differently, and a character may embrace, question, reject, or reinterpret parts of the culture they came from.

Culture helps explain what feels normal to your character, what skills they learned early, and what parts of the world feel familiar or unfamiliar. It gives you a grounded starting point, not a script.

### A Good Rule of Thumb

If a trait reflects something your character learned because of where and how they were raised, it probably belongs in culture.

If a trait reflects something physical, innate, ancestral, or biological, it probably belongs somewhere else.
