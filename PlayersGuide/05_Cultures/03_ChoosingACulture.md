## Choosing a Culture

Choose the culture that best reflects the kind of community and way of life that shaped your character before adventuring began.

Culture is about upbringing, not ancestry. When you choose a culture, think less about what your character is and more about how they were raised, what their daily life looked like, and what kinds of skills or habits they would have learned from the people around them.

A good way to choose a culture is to ask a few simple questions:

- Who raised your character?
- Where did they grow up?
- What kind of community did they belong to?
- What did daily life require them to learn?
- What customs, languages, or expectations were normal for them?

The answers do not need to be complicated. In most cases, your culture should reflect the environment and community that had the greatest influence on your character's early life.

For example, a character raised in a crowded trade city might choose the Cosmopolitan culture, even if their lineage is usually associated with some other traditional homeland. A character raised in a remote settlement at the edge of civilization might instead choose a culture shaped by hardship, travel, watchfulness, and self-reliance.

### Your Culture Does Not Decide Everything

Culture helps describe your starting point, but it does not define your entire identity.

Two characters from the same culture can still have very different personalities, beliefs, goals, and life stories. One cosmopolitan character might be outgoing and socially confident, while another might be cautious, observant, and deeply private. They share a background, not a destiny.

In the same way, a character may not fit every stereotype others associate with their culture. Your character is an individual. Culture explains what kind of world shaped them, not every choice they will make.

### Mixed Backgrounds

Some characters are shaped by more than one culture.

A child might be born in one community, raised in another, and spend adolescence traveling between both. Another character might grow up in a household that preserved one set of customs while living inside a very different wider society.

In cases like these, choose the culture that had the strongest day-to-day influence on your character, or the one that best represents the traits you want your character's upbringing to have provided.

If no single culture feels like a perfect fit, work with your Narrator to adapt one of the existing cultures or create a version that better matches your character's background.

### Choosing for the Story You Want

Culture is also a roleplaying choice.

It helps show what your character finds familiar, what parts of the world feel strange to them, what kinds of people they understand easily, and what assumptions they may carry into the story. Choosing a culture gives you an easy way to anchor your character in the setting and make them feel like they came from a real place with real influences.

When in doubt, choose the culture that feels most true to your character's lived experience.
