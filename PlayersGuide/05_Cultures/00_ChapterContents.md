Yes — I would **keep the term Lineages**.

For OFER, I think the best pairing is:

- **Lineage** = what you are by birth/inheritance
    
- **Culture** = the people and way of life that raised you
    

That pair is clear, elegant, and already very close to how you are structuring the game.

## My view on the terminology

### Lineage

**Pros**

- fantasy-facing and natural in tone
    
- clearly distinct from Culture
    
- broad enough to handle biological ancestry, supernatural inheritance, constructed peoples, mixed ancestry, and similar cases
    
- already established in your current work, so keeping it avoids churn
    

**Cons**

- slightly softer/less instantly literal than Species
    

Overall, I think the pros easily win.

### Species

**Pros**

- the clearest in a strictly literal sense
    
- immediately understandable to many readers
    

**Cons**

- feels more scientific than mythic/fantasy
    
- can sound sterile or modern in a way that cuts against the tone of a classic fantasy RPG
    
- can get awkward around edge cases like planar-touched peoples, magical transformations, descended-from-but-not-biologically-clean categories, and fantasy metaphysics generally
    
- some players simply do not like how it sounds in this genre
    

To me, **Species is clearer but worse**.

### Origins

**Pros**

- broad and flexible
    
- can encompass birth, upbringing, and formative background
    

**Cons**

- too broad for your structure
    
- becomes muddy once you already have Culture
    
- does not cleanly separate inherited traits from learned social traits
    
- if Chapter 4 were called Origins and Chapter 5 were Cultures, players might reasonably wonder what belongs where
    

So I agree with you: **Origins is less clear than Lineage** for this project.

## My recommendation

Lock in:

- **Chapter 4: Lineages**
    
- **Chapter 5: Cultures**
    

That gives OFER a very strong two-part character foundation:

- **Lineage** answers: “What people or inherited kind are you?”
    
- **Culture** answers: “What sort of community and upbringing shaped you?”
    

That is intuitive, player-friendly, and durable.

## Why this is especially good for OFER

This pairing supports a lot of good design behavior:

- it encourages **mix-and-match character building**
    
- it avoids collapsing culture into ancestry
    
- it keeps the system flexible for future expansions
    
- it works for both classic fantasy and more unusual character concepts
    
- it fits your chapter philosophy of one chapter per main topic
    

It also sounds like a real fantasy game rather than a technical taxonomy.
