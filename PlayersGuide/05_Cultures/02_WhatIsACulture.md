## What Is a Culture?

A culture is the set of habits, customs, skills, expectations, and ways of living that a character learned from the people and communities around them.

Culture reflects upbringing rather than ancestry. It helps answer questions such as these:

- Where was your character raised?
- What kind of community shaped them?
- What did everyday life teach them?
- What customs, languages, or practical skills did they grow up with?
- How do they understand the wider world?

In OFER, culture represents learned traits. These traits come from family life, local traditions, education, labor, religion, travel, trade, survival, social expectations, and the physical environment in which a character grew up.

A culture can be shared by people of many different lineages. A dwarf, elf, human, or any other character might all come from the same cosmopolitan city, the same frontier settlement, or the same seafaring community. They may look different, but they can still share many of the same learned traits because they were shaped by a similar way of life.

Likewise, characters of the same lineage can come from very different cultures. Two siblings separated early in life might grow into very different people if they are raised in different places, among different communities, under different customs and expectations.

Culture is not destiny. It does not lock a character's personality, beliefs, or choices into a single mold. Instead, it describes the kind of background that most strongly shaped the character before play begins. It gives you a practical starting point for understanding what your character knows, how they move through the world, and what kinds of situations feel familiar to them.

In game terms, a culture grants traits that reflect learned experience. These often include practical knowledge, environmental familiarity, social habits, trained abilities, and languages. Culture helps define what your character picked up from life before becoming an adventurer.

### Culture and Lineage

Culture and lineage describe different parts of a character.

Lineage covers traits tied to physical nature, ancestry, or inherited qualities.

Culture covers traits tied to upbringing, community, training, customs, and daily life.

Both matter, but they answer different questions. Keeping them separate makes characters easier to understand, easier to build, and more flexible to imagine.

### Example

A character with an elf lineage might be raised in a cosmopolitan trade city, a remote forest settlement, a military border town, or a monastery. Each of those lives would teach different habits, skills, and expectations.

Another character with an orc lineage might be raised in that same trade city and share much of the same culture as the elf. Their lineages differ, but their upbringing may have given them similar knowledge of crowds, languages, and urban life.

This is why OFER treats culture as something learned rather than inherited.
