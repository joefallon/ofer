## Determine Ability Scores

Your character’s **ability scores** describe their natural strengths and weaknesses. These scores affect nearly everything your character does, from fighting and sneaking to noticing danger and casting spells.

Every character has six ability scores:

- **Strength (STR)**, which measures physical power
- **Dexterity (DEX)**, which measures agility, balance, and quick reactions
- **Constitution (CON)**, which measures toughness and endurance
- **Intelligence (INT)**, which measures reasoning and learned knowledge
- **Wisdom (WIS)**, which measures awareness, judgment, and intuition
- **Charisma (CHA)**, which measures force of personality, confidence, and social presence

A higher score means your character is better in that area. An average person has a score of **10** in each ability. Adventurers are above average, which is one reason they can do extraordinary things.

As your character gains levels, some of these scores may improve. However, a player character cannot normally have an ability score higher than **20**.

### Why Ability Scores Matter

Ability scores are important because they affect many core parts of the game, including:

- **attack rolls**
- **damage rolls**
- **saving throws**
- **skill checks**
- **spellcasting**
- **hit points**, through Constitution

Some classes rely more heavily on certain abilities than others. For example:

- A warrior who fights with heavy weapons usually wants a high **Strength**.
- A nimble archer or sneaky rogue usually wants a high **Dexterity**.
- A tough front-line character benefits from strong **Constitution**.
- A wizard depends on **Intelligence**.
- A perceptive priest or druid often values **Wisdom**.
- A persuasive leader or magical performer often relies on **Charisma**.

If you are new to the game, a good rule is to make your class’s main ability one of your highest scores.

### Three Common Methods

There are three popular ways to determine your starting ability scores:

- **rolling**
- **point-buy**
- **standard array**

No method is automatically better than the others. They simply create different experiences.

- **Rolling** is exciting and unpredictable.
- **Point-buy** gives you the most control.
- **Standard array** is the fastest and simplest.

Your GM may choose which method the group uses. If not, choose the one that sounds most fun to you.

### Method 1: Rolling

This is the classic method. It adds randomness and can lead to surprising characters.

To roll your ability scores:

1. Roll **four six-sided dice (4d6)**.
2. Ignore the **lowest** die.
3. Add the remaining three dice together.
4. Write down the total.
5. Repeat this process until you have **six totals**.
6. Assign those six numbers to your six ability scores in any order you like.
7. After that, add:
    - **+2 to one score of your choice that is 16 or lower**
    - **+1 to one score of your choice that is 17 or lower**

This method can create strong characters, uneven characters, or characters with unusual strengths and weaknesses.

#### Rolling Example

Suppose you roll these six totals:

- **15**
- **14**
- **13**
- **12**
- **10**
- **8**

You decide to play a strong fighter, so you assign them like this:

- **STR 15**
- **DEX 12**
- **CON 14**
- **INT 8**
- **WIS 10**
- **CHA 13**

Then you apply the extra bonuses:

- Add **+2** to Strength, raising it from **15 to 17**
- Add **+1** to Constitution, raising it from **14 to 15**

Your final scores become:

- **STR 17**
- **DEX 12**
- **CON 15**
- **INT 8**
- **WIS 10**
- **CHA 13**

This gives you a character who is strong and durable, which fits the concept well.

### Method 2: Point-Buy

If you want full control and no randomness, use point-buy.

With this method, you begin with **32 points** to spend on your six ability scores.

Important rules:

- **18** is the highest score you can start with
- **8** is the lowest score you can have
- you buy one score for each of the six abilities
- you do **not** add any extra bonuses at the end

Use the following table to see how many points each score costs.

### Point-Buy Cost

|Score|Cost|Score|Cost|
|---|---|---|---|
|8|0|14|7|
|9|1|15|9|
|10|2|16|11|
|11|3|17|13|
|12|4|18|16|
|13|5|||

Point-buy is useful if you want to build exactly the character you have in mind and avoid the chance of unusually high or low rolls.

#### Point-Buy Example

Suppose you want to create a wizard. You want high Intelligence, decent Dexterity, and solid Constitution.

You might buy:

- **STR 8** for **0**
- **DEX 14** for **7**
- **CON 14** for **7**
- **INT 18** for **16**
- **WIS 10** for **2**
- **CHA 8** for **0**

Total cost: **32**

This gives you a very smart wizard with decent toughness and quickness, while leaving Strength and Charisma lower.

### Method 3: Standard Array

If you want to start quickly, use the standard array.

The standard array is:

- **16**
- **14**
- **14**
- **13**
- **10**
- **8**

Assign these six numbers to your six ability scores in any order you like. Do **not** modify them further.

This method is fast, balanced, and easy for new players.

#### Standard Array Example

Suppose you want to play a cleric who wears armor, supports allies, and survives danger.

You might assign the standard array like this:

- **STR 13**
- **DEX 10**
- **CON 14**
- **INT 8**
- **WIS 16**
- **CHA 14**

This gives you high Wisdom for spellcasting, good Constitution for toughness, and decent Charisma for leading or comforting others.

### Which Method Should You Choose?

If you are not sure, here is a simple guide:

- Choose **rolling** if you enjoy randomness and discovering your character as you build them.
- Choose **point-buy** if you want precise control.
- Choose **standard array** if you want the fastest and easiest option.

For many new players, **standard array** is the simplest place to start. It is quick, balanced, and makes it easier to focus on learning the rest of the game.

### Assigning Your Scores

Once you know your six numbers, assign them to the six abilities in whatever order best fits your character.

A helpful beginner approach is:

1. Put your **highest score** in the ability your class uses most.
2. Put a good score in **Constitution**, because extra toughness helps almost every character.
3. Put your next-best scores where they support your concept.
4. Let your lowest score reflect a weakness or limitation.

A weakness does not make a character bad. It often makes a character more interesting.

For example:

- A brave knight might have low Intelligence because they are not well educated.
- A brilliant wizard might have low Strength because they avoid physical labor.
- A charming scoundrel might have low Wisdom because they act before thinking.

### Find Ability Modifiers

After you assign your scores, determine your **ability modifiers**.

Ability modifiers are the numbers you will use most often during play. When the game asks you to make an attack roll, skill check, saving throw, or spell attack, you usually add one of these modifiers.

Use the table below.

### Ability Modifiers

|Ability Score|Modifier|Ability Score|Modifier|
|---|---|---|---|
|1|-5|12-13|+1|
|2-3|-4|14-15|+2|
|4-5|-3|16-17|+3|
|6-7|-2|18-19|+4|
|8-9|-1|20|+5|
|10-11|+0|||

#### Modifier Example

If your character has:

- **Strength 17**, the modifier is **+3**
- **Dexterity 14**, the modifier is **+2**
- **Constitution 10**, the modifier is **+0**
- **Intelligence 8**, the modifier is **-1**

You will usually write both the score and the modifier on your character sheet.

### A Helpful Final Check

Before moving on, make sure you have done all of the following:

- **chosen one method for generating scores**
- **determined all six ability scores**
- **assigned each score to an ability**
- **calculated each ability modifier**
- **written both scores and modifiers on your character sheet**

Once that is done, you are ready to choose your lineage.
