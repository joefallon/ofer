## Multiclassing: XP, Hit Points, Hit Dice, and Proficiency Bonus

Once you qualify for multiclassing, a few core rules determine how your character advances overall.

These rules answer four practical questions:

- How do you know when you level up?
- How many hit points do you gain?
- How do your hit dice work?
- What proficiency bonus do you use?

The key idea is simple: some parts of your character are based on your total level, while others depend on the specific class you just advanced in.

## Experience Points

Your XP progression is always based on your total character level, not your level in any one class.

Your character level is the sum of all your class levels combined. When you earn enough XP to reach the next character level, you gain one new level. At that point, you choose which class receives that level.

This means multiclassing does not create separate XP tracks for different classes. You do not level your classes independently.

### Example: XP Uses Total Character Level

Tamsin is a 6th-level cleric and a 1st-level fighter.

That means Tamsin is a 7th-level character overall.

To gain another level, Tamsin must earn enough XP to reach 8th level. Once that happens, Tamsin can choose either:

- cleric 7/fighter 1, or
- cleric 6/fighter 2

The XP requirement is based on total level 7 to 8, not on the next level of cleric or fighter by themselves.

## Hit Points

When you gain a level in a new class, you gain hit points for that class the same way you do when gaining a level after 1st level.

Roll the new class's hit die, add your Constitution modifier, and add the result to your maximum hit points. If your group uses average hit points instead of rolling, use the average value for that class's hit die and add your Constitution modifier.

This is important: when you take your first level in a second class, you do not use that class's special 1st-level hit point calculation. That rule is only for the class you began the game in at 1st level.

### Example: First Level in a New Class

Bran begins as a fighter and later multiclasses into wizard.

When Bran takes wizard 1 as a multiclass level, Bran does not gain the full 1st-level wizard hit point amount.

Instead, Bran gains hit points the same way any character does after 1st level: by rolling 1d6 and adding Constitution, or by taking the average for a wizard hit die and adding Constitution.

## Hit Dice

Each class grants hit dice based on that class's hit die size. When you multiclass, all of those hit dice become part of your hit dice pool.

If two classes use the same die type, you can pool those dice together. If they use different die types, track them separately.

### Example: Same Hit Die Type

Ela is a 5th-level paladin and a 5th-level fighter.

Both classes use d10 hit dice.

Ela therefore has ten d10 hit dice.

### Example: Different Hit Die Types

Neris is a 5th-level paladin and a 5th-level cleric.

Paladin uses d10 hit dice, while cleric uses d8 hit dice.

Neris therefore has:

- five d10 hit dice
- five d8 hit dice

These are tracked separately.

## Proficiency Bonus

Your proficiency bonus is always based on your total character level, not your level in an individual class.

This is one of the most important multiclassing rules to remember. Even if your class levels are split, your overall adventuring experience still improves your general competence.

### Example: Proficiency Bonus by Total Level

Kestrel is a 3rd-level fighter and a 2nd-level rogue.

That makes Kestrel a 5th-level character overall.

Kestrel uses the proficiency bonus of a 5th-level character, which is +3.

It does not matter that neither class is 5th level on its own.

## The Big Pattern

Multiclassing mixes two kinds of progression:

### Based on Total Character Level

These use your combined levels across all classes:

- XP thresholds for leveling up
- proficiency bonus

### Based on the Class You Just Advanced In

These use the specific class level you gained:

- hit points gained from the new level
- hit die added from that class

This pattern shows up again in later multiclassing rules. Total level often governs broad character progress, while class level governs class-specific gains.

## Example: Putting It All Together

Marra is currently rogue 4/wizard 2.

That means:

- character level 6
- rogue level 4
- wizard level 2

When Marra earns enough XP to reach character level 7, she gains one level.

Because XP is based on total character level, she advances when a 6th-level character reaches the threshold for 7th level.

She then chooses which class gains the level.

If she becomes rogue 5/wizard 2:

- her character level becomes 7
- she gains rogue 5 class benefits
- she adds one rogue hit die
- she gains hit points based on the rogue hit die
- her proficiency bonus is based on total character level 7

If she becomes rogue 4/wizard 3 instead:

- her character level still becomes 7
- she gains wizard 3 class benefits
- she adds one wizard hit die
- she gains hit points based on the wizard hit die
- her proficiency bonus is still based on total character level 7

The overall level is the same either way. The class benefits and hit die depend on which class receives the new level.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

A few multiclassing mistakes are especially easy to make:

- treating each class as if it had its own separate XP track
- using a new class's full 1st-level hit point rule when taking that class as a multiclass level
- forgetting to combine hit dice from all classes
- calculating proficiency bonus from class level instead of total character level

Keeping total level and class level clearly separated will prevent most confusion.

## Quick Reference

For multiclass characters:

- XP progression is based on total character level
- when you level up, you choose which class gains the new level
- hit points for a multiclass level are gained as a level after 1st, even if it is your first level in that class
- add hit dice from all classes to your hit dice pool
- pool same-size hit dice together and track different sizes separately
- proficiency bonus is based on total character level

## Multiclassing Proficiencies

Taking your first level in a new class gives you access to that class's training, but not all of it.

A character who starts in a class receives the full package of that class's starting proficiencies. A character who multiclasses into that class later gains only a limited set of additional proficiencies. This reflects the difference between beginning your adventuring life with that training and picking up a second discipline along the way.

In practical terms, multiclassing gives you some of the new class's basic tools, but not everything.

## The Core Rule

When you gain your first level in a class other than your initial class, you gain only the proficiencies listed for that class in the Multiclassing Proficiencies table.

You do not automatically gain all of the class's starting proficiencies.

You also do not gain the class's starting equipment.

This rule applies only when you take your first level in that new class. After that, further levels in the class grant whatever features the class says they grant, but they do not give you another round of starting proficiencies.

## Multiclassing Proficiencies Table

|Class|Proficiencies Gained|
|---|---|
|Barbarian|Shields, simple weapons, martial weapons|
|Bard|Light armor, one tool of your choice|
|Cleric|Light armor, medium armor, shields|
|Druid|Light armor, medium armor, shields|
|Fighter|Light armor, medium armor, shields, simple weapons, martial weapons|
|Mechanist|Light armor, medium armor, shields, simple weapons, martial weapons|
|Monk|Simple weapons, shortswords|
|Paladin|Light armor, medium armor, shields, simple weapons, martial weapons|
|Ranger|Light armor, medium armor, shields, simple weapons, martial weapons|
|Rogue|Light armor, one tool of your choice|
|Sorcerer|None|
|Warlock|Light armor, simple weapons|
|Wizard|None|

## Why This Rule Exists

Multiclassing is about broadening your character, not rebuilding them from the ground up.

A 1st-level fighter begins with the full training expected of someone whose entire starting background supports that class. A wizard who later takes a fighter level is learning to fight seriously, but is not being treated as though they began life with the complete fighter package.

This helps keep multiclassing flavorful and flexible without letting it replace the importance of your starting class.

## Example: Wizard Multiclassing into Fighter

Talan is a wizard who later takes a level in fighter.

Because this is Talan's first fighter level, Talan gains the proficiencies listed for fighter in the multiclass table:

- light armor
- medium armor
- shields
- simple weapons
- martial weapons

Talan does not gain every fighter starting proficiency unless the rules specifically say so. The multiclass table is what matters.

## Example: Rogue Multiclassing into Cleric

Sera is a rogue who later takes a level in cleric.

When Sera gains cleric 1 as a multiclass level, she gains:

- light armor
- medium armor
- shields

She does not gain full cleric starting proficiencies beyond what the multiclass table provides.

## Example: Fighter Multiclassing into Wizard

Dain is a fighter who later takes a level in wizard.

The multiclass table gives wizard no additional proficiencies.

That means Dain gains wizard class features as normal for that level, but gains no new armor, weapon, or tool proficiencies from multiclassing into wizard.

## Tool Choices Matter

Some classes grant "one tool of your choice" when taken as a multiclass.

This means the player chooses a specific tool proficiency at the time the class is gained. Make that choice deliberately. A tool can add flavor, utility, and problem-solving options that help define how the new class fits your character.

For example, a rogue multiclass might choose thieves' tools if not already proficient, or a disguise kit, forgery kit, or another tool that fits the character's evolving role.

## What You Do Not Get

When multiclassing into a new class, remember what you are not receiving automatically:

- the full starting proficiency list of that class
- the class's starting equipment
- a second copy of anything you already have just because the new class also grants it

If you already have a proficiency, gaining it again does not usually provide an extra benefit.

## A Good Character-Building Question

When considering a multiclass, ask not only "What features do I want?" but also "What training does this actually add?"

Sometimes the added proficiencies are a major part of the appeal. A lightly armored character may value gaining shields and medium armor. A martial character may value access to new weapons. In other cases, the class features matter far more than the proficiency list.

Looking at both together helps you understand what the multiclass level really gives you.

## Quick Reference

When you take your first level in a new class:

- use the Multiclassing Proficiencies table
- gain only the proficiencies listed there
- do not gain the class's full starting proficiencies
- do not gain the class's starting equipment
