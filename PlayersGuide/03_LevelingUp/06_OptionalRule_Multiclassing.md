## Optional Rule: Multiclassing

Not every adventurer follows a straight path.

Some characters begin as soldiers and later study magic. Some start as thieves and become holy champions. Some discover that one class alone does not fully capture who they are becoming. Multiclassing exists for those characters.

Multiclassing is an optional rule that allows you to gain levels in more than one class. Instead of advancing only in your current class, you can take a level in a different class when you level up, if your GM allows it.

This rule gives you more flexibility, more combinations, and more ways to realize a specific character concept.

## What Multiclassing Lets You Do

Multiclassing lets you combine features from different classes.

A character might:

- begin as a fighter and later learn wizardry
- start as a rogue and take levels in ranger
- blend cleric and paladin levels to create a more martial divine character
- dip into another class for a small set of useful features
- split advancement across several classes to build a very specific style of adventurer

This added flexibility comes with a tradeoff. Multiclass characters gain versatility, but they give up some focus. A single-class character reaches higher-level class features sooner. A multiclass character spreads that growth across multiple paths.

## The GM Decides

Because multiclassing is optional, it is only used if the GM allows it.

Some campaigns welcome it freely. Others limit it or leave it out entirely. Before planning a multiclass character, make sure your group is actually using this rule.

## Character Level and Class Levels

When you multiclass, you track both your level in each class and your total level across all classes.

Your total combined levels are called your character level.

Example: If you have 3 levels in wizard and 2 levels in fighter, you are a 5th-level character.

That does not mean you are a 5th-level wizard or a 5th-level fighter. It means your total character level is 5, made up of wizard 3 and fighter 2.

This distinction matters because some rules use your total character level, while others use your level in a specific class.

## How Multiclassing Works

When your character gains a level, you normally take the next level in your current class.

If multiclassing is allowed, you can instead take a level in a different class, provided you meet that class's requirements. When you do, you gain the benefits of that class level rather than the next level of your original class.

You keep all the levels you already have. You are adding a new class level, not replacing an old one.

Example: A 4th-level fighter gains a level. Instead of becoming a 5th-level fighter, the character takes 1 level in rogue. The character is now fighter 4/rogue 1, with a total character level of 5.

## Multiclassing Can Change a Character's Direction

Some characters multiclass once and then return to their original class. Others split levels more evenly. Others change course entirely.

A character might begin as a wizard and later take several fighter levels after surviving a brutal war. Another might begin as a ranger, take one rogue level for expertise and stealth, then continue as a ranger for the rest of the campaign.

There is no single correct pattern. Multiclassing can reflect training, transformation, crisis, ambition, or a long-term character concept that one class alone does not fully express.

## Example: A Small Dip

Seren is a 5th-level cleric. After several adventures spent sneaking through ruins and dealing with traps, the player decides that rogue better fits the character's growth.

When Seren next gains a level, instead of becoming cleric 6, Seren takes rogue 1.

Seren is now a 6th-level character overall: cleric 5/rogue 1.

Seren gains the 1st-level benefits of rogue as allowed by the multiclass rules, but does not gain the 6th-level cleric benefits yet.

## Example: A True Hybrid

Varek begins as a fighter but later studies arcane magic.

By mid-campaign, Varek is fighter 3/wizard 3.

Varek is a 6th-level character overall, but class features are determined separately. Fighter features depend on fighter level 3. Wizard features depend on wizard level 3.

This means Varek gains a broader mix of abilities, but reaches higher-level fighter and wizard features more slowly than a single-class character would.

## Why Players Choose Multiclassing

Players usually multiclass for one of three reasons:

- to match a strong character concept
- to add useful mechanical options
- to create a hybrid playstyle that one class does not provide on its own

Done well, multiclassing can make a character feel highly personal. It can also make leveling decisions more strategic, since every new level involves choosing between deepening one path or branching farther into another.

## A Word of Caution

Multiclassing is powerful, but it is not automatically better.

A multiclass character often gains flexibility at the cost of delayed progression. You may reach key class features later than a single-class character, and some combinations are smoother than others.

For that reason, multiclassing works best when you have a clear idea of what you want the character to become.

## Quick Reference

With multiclassing:

- you can take levels in more than one class
- your total levels across all classes equal your character level
- your level in each individual class still matters separately
- when you gain a level, you can take it in a new class instead of your current one, if your GM allows it
- multiclassing usually trades focus for versatility
