## Chapter 3: Leveling Up

Adventurers do not stay the same for long.

Each expedition, narrow escape, hard-won victory, and painful mistake shapes your character into something more capable than before. As you gain levels, you become tougher, more skilled, and more dangerous. Your class grants new features, your hit points increase, and your place in the world becomes more significant.

This chapter explains how leveling up works.

Inside, you will find the core rules for gaining levels, the two main methods groups use to track advancement, and the optional multiclassing rules for characters who blend the strengths of more than one class.

## What This Chapter Covers

This chapter is organized into three main topics:

### Gaining Levels

These rules explain what happens when your character advances, including class benefits, character level, hit dice, and hit points.

### Leveling Methods

Some groups track advancement with experience points, while others level up through milestones tied to major goals in the campaign. Both approaches are presented here.

### Optional Rule: Multiclassing

If your group uses multiclassing, this chapter explains how to qualify for a new class and how mixed class levels affect hit points, proficiencies, class features, and spellcasting.

## Character Level and Class Level

Most characters advance in a single class, so their class level and character level are the same.

If you use multiclassing, those two numbers can differ. Your class level is your level in a specific class. Your character level is the total of all your class levels added together.

A 5th-level fighter has a character level of 5. A character with 3 levels in wizard and 2 levels in fighter is also 5th level overall, even though they are not 5th level in either class.

This distinction matters because some game rules care about your total character level, while others care about your level in a particular class.

## A Note for Players and GMs

Leveling up should feel rewarding.

For players, it is a chance to see your character grow from a fragile beginner into a seasoned adventurer. For GMs, it is a pacing tool that helps shape the rhythm of a campaign. Faster advancement can create a sweeping heroic arc, while slower advancement gives more room for each level to breathe.

Whichever method your group uses, the goal is the same: character growth that feels earned, exciting, and easy to follow.
