## Examples of Leveling and Multiclassing

Rules are easiest to learn when you can see them in motion.

The examples below show how the chapter's rules work at the table. Each one focuses on a common question that comes up when characters gain levels, track advancement, or branch into multiple classes.

Use these examples as models when you need a quick answer during play.

## Example 1: Gaining a Level in a Single Class

Rina is a 2nd-level fighter.

After the session, her group gains enough advancement to reach 3rd level. Rina takes her next level in fighter.

Rina becomes a 3rd-level fighter. Her character level is now 3, her fighter level is now 3, she gains the class benefits listed for fighter at 3rd level, she adds 1 fighter hit die, and her maximum hit points increase based on that new fighter hit die.

This is the standard pattern for leveling up in one class.

## Example 2: Reading the XP Threshold

Daren has 2,650 XP.

The Level Advancement table shows that 4th level begins at 2,700 XP.

That means Daren is still 3rd level. Once Daren reaches 2,700 XP, Daren becomes 4th level.

The table shows total XP needed for each level, not the amount you gain after resetting from the previous one.

## Example 3: XP Is Cumulative

Tessa reaches 300 XP and becomes 2nd level.

Later, Tessa reaches 900 XP and becomes 3rd level.

Tessa does not reset to 0 XP after reaching 2nd level. Her XP total keeps increasing across the campaign.

This is true whether she remains in one class or later multiclasses.

## Example 4: Milestone Advancement

The party is sent to rescue prisoners from a ruined fort.

They scout the site, bypass alarms, defeat the guards, and lead the prisoners safely home.

The GM decides that this mission was a major goal in the campaign and awards a level.

No XP total is needed. The characters level because they reached an important milestone.

## Example 5: Character Level and Class Level

Joren has 3 levels in wizard and 2 levels in fighter.

That means:

- wizard level 3
- fighter level 2
- character level 5

Joren is not a 5th-level wizard and is not a 5th-level fighter. Joren is a 5th-level character made up of those two class levels.

Whenever a rule refers to total character level, use 5. Whenever a rule refers to wizard level or fighter level, use the level in that specific class.

## Example 6: Taking a Level in a New Class

Mira is a 4th-level rogue.

When she gains her next level, she chooses to take 1 level in ranger instead of becoming rogue 5.

Mira is now rogue 4/ranger 1.

Her character level is 5 because her class levels add up to 5. She gains the 1st-level ranger benefits allowed by the multiclassing rules, not the 5th-level rogue benefits she would have received if she had stayed single-classed.

## Example 7: Multiclass Prerequisites

Halan is a barbarian with Strength 15 and Wisdom 12.

Halan wants to take a level in druid.

To do that, Halan must meet the prerequisite for barbarian and the prerequisite for druid:

- barbarian requires STR 13
- druid requires WIS 13

Halan meets the barbarian requirement but not the druid requirement. Halan cannot multiclass into druid yet.

## Example 8: Hit Points for a Multiclass Level

Sera begins as a fighter and later takes a level in wizard.

When Sera gains wizard 1 as a multiclass level, she does not use the special 1st-level wizard hit point rule.

Instead, she gains hit points the same way characters do after 1st level: she rolls 1d6 and adds her Constitution modifier, or takes the average value for the wizard hit die and adds her Constitution modifier.

This is true even though it is her first wizard level.

## Example 9: Combining Hit Dice

Kellan is a 5th-level paladin and a 5th-level fighter.

Both classes use d10 hit dice, so Kellan has ten d10 hit dice.

Lysa is a 5th-level paladin and a 5th-level cleric.

Paladin uses d10 hit dice and cleric uses d8 hit dice, so Lysa has:

- five d10 hit dice
- five d8 hit dice

Same-size hit dice can be pooled together. Different-size hit dice are tracked separately.

## Example 10: Proficiency Bonus Uses Total Character Level

Nora is a 3rd-level fighter and a 2nd-level rogue.

That makes Nora a 5th-level character.

Her proficiency bonus is the bonus for character level 5, which is +3.

It does not matter that neither fighter nor rogue has reached 5th level on its own.

## Example 11: Multiclass Proficiencies

Tal is a wizard who takes a level in fighter.

Because this is Tal's first fighter level taken through multiclassing, Tal gains only the proficiencies listed for fighter in the multiclassing proficiencies table.

Tal does not gain the full fighter starting package unless the rules specifically say so.

Likewise, Tal does not gain fighter starting equipment.

## Example 12: Channel Divinity Across Classes

A character is cleric 6/paladin 4.

This character has Channel Divinity from more than one class. The character gains access to Channel Divinity effects from both classes, but does not add together separate pools of uses.

If the cleric level is high enough to grant two uses, then the character has two uses total, not two from cleric plus another from paladin.

When using Channel Divinity, the character may choose any eligible Channel Divinity effect gained from either class.

## Example 13: Multiattack Does Not Stack

Vera is fighter 9/monk 5.

Suppose fighter grants a version of Multiattack that allows three attacks, while monk grants a version that allows two attacks.

Vera does not make five attacks by combining them.

Instead, Vera uses the best applicable version available. Multiattack features from different classes do not stack together.

## Example 14: Unarmored Defense Does Not Stack

Orin begins as a barbarian and later multiclasses into monk.

Both classes can grant Unarmored Defense.

Orin does not gain two versions of Unarmored Defense to combine. Orin uses the version from the class chosen at 1st level.

Because Orin began as a barbarian, Orin uses the barbarian version.

## Example 15: Spells Known Stay Class-Based

Elda is ranger 4/wizard 3.

Elda determines ranger spells from ranger level 4 and wizard spells from wizard level 3.

Elda does not prepare or know spells as though she were a 7th-level wizard or a 7th-level ranger.

Her spell selection remains split by class even though her character level is 7.

## Example 16: Spellcasting Ability Stays Class-Based

Selene is cleric 3/wizard 3.

When Selene casts a cleric spell, she uses the spellcasting ability for cleric spells.

When Selene casts a wizard spell, she uses the spellcasting ability for wizard spells.

The spell's class determines which spellcasting ability applies.

## Example 17: Spell Slots Are Combined

Aric is ranger 4/wizard 3.

To determine spell slots:

- wizard contributes all 3 levels
- ranger contributes half of 4, rounded down, which is 2

This gives a total of 5 for multiclass spell slot progression.

Using the multiclass spell slot table, Aric has:

- four 1st-circle slots
- three 2nd-circle slots
- two 3rd-circle slots

These are Aric's spell slots, even though Aric's actual class levels are ranger 4 and wizard 3.

## Example 18: Higher-Level Slots Without Higher-Level Spells

Continue with Aric, who is ranger 4/wizard 3.

Aric has 3rd-circle spell slots from multiclass spell slot progression.

However, Aric does not automatically know or prepare 3rd-circle spells just because those slots exist.

Aric can use those 3rd-circle slots to cast lower-circle spells. If one of those spells improves when cast with a higher-circle slot, that improved effect applies.

The spell slots are shared. The spells known remain class-based.

## Example 19: Choosing Where the New Level Goes

Marra is rogue 4/wizard 2.

After earning enough advancement, Marra reaches character level 7.

At that point, Marra gains one level and chooses where it goes.

If Marra becomes rogue 5/wizard 2, she gains rogue 5 features, a rogue hit die, and hit points based on the rogue hit die.

If Marra becomes rogue 4/wizard 3, she gains wizard 3 features, a wizard hit die, and hit points based on the wizard hit die.

Either way, Marra becomes a 7th-level character. The difference is which class receives the new level.

## Example 20: The Big Rule of Multiclassing

Tovin is cleric 5/fighter 3.

Tovin's total character level is 8.

From that one fact, several things follow:

- XP progression is based on being an 8th-level character
- proficiency bonus is based on total level 8
- cleric features are based on cleric level 5
- fighter features are based on fighter level 3
- hit dice include both cleric and fighter hit dice
- if Tovin gains another level, Tovin chooses which class receives it

This is the core logic of multiclassing in one example: total character level governs overall progression, while class level governs class-specific benefits.

## Using These Examples at the Table

If a multiclass rule ever feels confusing, start by asking two questions:

### What Is the Character's Total Level?

Use this for things like XP progression and proficiency bonus.

### What Is the Character's Level in Each Class?

Use this for things like class features, spells known or prepared, hit die gained, and other class-specific benefits.

That simple distinction resolves most multiclass questions quickly.

## Quick Summary

These examples show the main patterns of the chapter:

- leveling up grants new class benefits, hit points, and a hit die
- XP thresholds are cumulative
- milestones replace XP with goal-based advancement
- multiclassing combines class levels into one total character level
- total character level and class level are not the same thing
- hit dice, class features, and spells are often tracked by class
- proficiency bonus and XP progression use total character level
- multiclass spell slots are shared, but spells known or prepared remain class-based

With these examples in mind, you should be able to apply the chapter's rules with much more confidence during play.
