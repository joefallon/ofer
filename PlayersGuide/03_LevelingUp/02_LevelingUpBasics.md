## Gaining a Level

Adventuring changes people.

A goblin ambush teaches lessons that training yards do not. A desperate climb out of a trapped ruin leaves hard-earned instincts behind. A brush with death makes even a novice a little tougher, a little sharper, and a lot harder to stop.

When your character gains a level, that growth becomes part of the rules.

## What You Gain When You Level Up

When you gain a new level, you receive the benefits listed for your class at that level.

These benefits usually include some combination of the following:

- new class features
- improvements to existing class features
- a new hit die
- more hit points
- other class-based benefits shown on your class progression table

Your class tells you exactly what you gain at each level. When you reach a new level, check the entry for that level in your class.

## Character Level and Class Level

Two different level counts matter in the game.

### Character Level

Your character level is your total number of levels in all classes combined.

### Class Level

Your class level is your level in a specific class.

If you have only one class, these numbers are the same. If you use multiclassing, they can be different.

**Example:** A 4th-level rogue has character level 4 and rogue level 4.

**Example:** A character with 3 levels in wizard and 2 levels in fighter has character level 5, wizard level 3, and fighter level 2.

This distinction matters because some rules care about your total character level, while others care about your level in a particular class.

## Gaining a Hit Die

Each time you gain a level, you gain 1 hit die from the class you just advanced in.

This hit die is added to your pool of hit dice. Hit dice are useful during rests and also reflect your growing toughness as an adventurer.

A fighter who gains a level adds a d10 hit die. A cleric who gains a level adds a d8 hit die. A wizard who gains a level adds a d6 hit die.

## Gaining Hit Points

Each time you gain a level, your maximum hit points increase.

To determine how many hit points you gain, do the following:

1. Roll the new hit die for the class you gained.
2. Add your Constitution modifier.
3. Add the total to your maximum hit points.

Instead of rolling, you can take the average value listed for your class's hit die and add your Constitution modifier.

This choice lets groups decide whether leveling is a little unpredictable or fully consistent.

## Hit Point Example: Rolling

Tarin is a fighter with a Constitution modifier of +2. He reaches 2nd level.

Because he gained a level in fighter, he gains 1d10 hit points. He rolls the die and gets a 6. He adds his +2 Constitution modifier, for a total of 8.

His maximum hit points increase by 8.

## Hit Point Example: Taking the Average

Mira is a cleric with a Constitution modifier of +1. She reaches 4th level.

Instead of rolling, she takes the average value for her class hit die. Her class tells her what average to use. She adds her +1 Constitution modifier to that amount and increases her maximum hit points by the result.

No roll is needed.

## Your Class Determines Your Growth

Leveling up is not just about surviving longer. It is also how your character becomes more distinct.

A fighter may become a steadier front-line threat. A rogue may grow deadlier and more precise. A wizard may unlock more powerful magic. A ranger may deepen both martial skill and wilderness mastery.

Even before subclasses, levels shape the kind of hero your character is becoming. When you gain a level, you are not only adding numbers to a sheet. You are defining how your character has changed through play.

## Quick Reference

When your character gains a level:

- increase your class level in the class you advanced
- increase your character level by 1
- gain the benefits listed for that class level
- add 1 hit die from that class
- increase your maximum hit points
