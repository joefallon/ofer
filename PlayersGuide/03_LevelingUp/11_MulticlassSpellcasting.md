## Multiclass Spellcasting

Multiclass spellcasting is where many hybrid characters become truly interesting.

A warrior-scholar might blend steel and wizardry. A holy champion might combine divine power with another calling. A wilderness scout might add arcane study to primal instincts. Multiclass spellcasting makes these characters possible, but it also adds a few extra rules.

The core idea is simple:

- the spells you know or prepare are still tracked by class
- the spellcasting ability for each spell still comes from its class
- your spell slots may be combined across classes

In other words, multiclass spellcasters keep their class identities, but share a larger pool of magical fuel.

## When These Rules Apply

Use the rules in this section only if you have the Spellcasting feature from more than one class.

If you multiclass but have spellcasting from only one class, follow the normal spellcasting rules for that class and ignore the multiclass spellcasting rules here.

Example: A fighter who multiclasses into rogue but has no spellcasting feature follows no special spellcasting rules. A cleric who multiclasses into fighter but remains a spellcaster in only one class also just uses the normal cleric rules.

## The Three Parts of Multiclass Spellcasting

Multiclass spellcasting has three main parts:

### 1. Spells Known or Prepared

You determine spells known or prepared separately for each class.

### 2. Spellcasting Ability and Focus

Each spell remains tied to the class it came from.

### 3. Spell Slots

Your spell slots are determined by combining spellcasting levels from all relevant classes, then consulting the multiclass spell slot table.

These three parts work together, but they are not calculated the same way. That is what makes multiclass spellcasting feel more complex at first.

## Spells Known and Prepared

Determine what spells you know and can prepare for each class individually, as if you were a single-classed member of that class.

This is one of the most important rules in the section.

Your levels in one spellcasting class do not increase the number of spells known or prepared in another spellcasting class unless that class's own rules say so.

Example: If you are a 4th-level ranger and a 3rd-level wizard, you determine your ranger spells from ranger level 4 and your wizard spells from wizard level 3.

That means:

- your ranger spells known are based only on ranger 4
- your wizard cantrips, spellbook, and prepared spells are based only on wizard 3

You do not act like a 7th-level wizard or a 7th-level ranger for those purposes.

## Example: Ranger and Wizard

Arlen is ranger 4/wizard 3.

As a 4th-level ranger, Arlen knows the number of Primordial spells allowed by ranger level 4.

As a 3rd-level wizard, Arlen knows the number of Arcane cantrips allowed by wizard level 3, and the spellbook contains the number of Arcane spells allowed by wizard level 3. If wizard preparation is based on Intelligence, Arlen prepares wizard spells using the normal wizard rule for a 3rd-level wizard.

Even though Arlen is a 7th-level character overall, spell knowledge and preparation are still split by class.

## Each Spell Belongs to a Class

Every spell you know or prepare is associated with one of your classes.

That matters because each class may use:

- a different spellcasting ability
- a different spell list or source
- a different spellcasting focus

When you cast a spell, use the spellcasting ability of the class that gave you that spell.

A wizard spell uses Intelligence. A cleric spell uses the class ability for cleric spells. A ranger spell uses the class ability for ranger spells. The spell does not switch abilities just because you also have levels in another spellcasting class.

## Spellcasting Foci Stay Class-Specific

A spellcasting focus works only for spells tied to the class associated with that focus.

A holy symbol is used for the class that allows it. An arcane focus is used for the class that allows it. One class's focus does not automatically function for another class's spells unless a rule specifically says it does.

This is easy to overlook, but it matters for characters carrying more than one magical tradition.

## Example: Cleric and Wizard

Selene is cleric 3/wizard 3.

She prepares cleric spells as a 3rd-level cleric and wizard spells as a 3rd-level wizard.

When she casts a cleric spell, she uses her cleric spellcasting ability.

When she casts a wizard spell, she uses her wizard spellcasting ability.

If she is holding a holy symbol, that can serve as a focus only for her cleric spells. If she wants to use a focus for wizard spells, she needs the kind of focus her wizard class allows.

## Shared Spell Slots

While spells known and prepared stay separated by class, spell slots are combined.

To determine your spell slots, add together the following:

- all your levels in bard, cleric, druid, sorcerer, and wizard
- half your levels, rounded down, in paladin, ranger, and warlock
- one-third of your levels, rounded down, in a class that gains spellcasting only through a spellcasting subclass, such as a fighter subclass with spellcasting

Once you have that total, use it to determine your spell slots from the multiclass spellcaster spell slot table.

This total is not your character level unless your classes happen to make it so. It is your multiclass spellcaster level for slot progression.

## Example: Ranger 4 and Wizard 3

Suppose you are ranger 4/wizard 3.

To determine spell slots:

- wizard contributes all 3 levels
- ranger contributes half of 4, rounded down, which is 2

That gives a total of 5 for spell slot progression.

Consulting the table for 5th level gives:

- four 1st-circle slots
- three 2nd-circle slots
- two 3rd-circle slots

Those are your spell slots, even though your actual class levels are ranger 4 and wizard 3.

## Higher-Level Slots Do Not Automatically Grant Higher-Level Spells

A multiclass spellcaster can have spell slots of a higher circle than the spells they actually know or can prepare.

This is normal.

The spell slot table tells you what slots you have available. Your class levels determine what spells you know or can prepare.

So if the table gives you 3rd-circle spell slots, but none of your classes has reached the level needed to know or prepare 3rd-circle spells, you still have those slots. You just cannot use them to cast spells you do not know or have not prepared.

You can, however, use those higher-circle slots to cast lower-circle spells.

If a lower-circle spell becomes stronger when cast with a higher-circle slot, you gain that enhanced effect.

## Example: Higher Slots, Lower Spells

Return to Arlen, who is ranger 4/wizard 3.

Arlen's combined spell slot progression grants 3rd-circle slots.

But Arlen does not yet know any 3rd-circle wizard spells, and does not know any higher-circle ranger spells beyond what ranger 4 allows.

That means Arlen cannot suddenly prepare or cast 3rd-circle spells just because the slot table includes 3rd-circle slots.

However, Arlen can use those 3rd-circle slots to cast lower-circle spells. If one of those spells improves when cast using a higher-circle slot, it gains that enhanced effect.

## A Useful Way to Think About It

Multiclass spellcasting separates magic into two layers:

### Your Spell Repertoire

This is what you know or prepare. It is determined class by class.

### Your Magical Fuel

This is your spell slot pool. It is determined by your combined spellcasting progression.

That is why a multiclass spellcaster can have plenty of magical power in the form of spell slots, while still having a limited menu of actual spells available from each class.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Multiclass spellcasting becomes much easier when you avoid a few common mistakes:

- treating spells known or prepared as if they were based on total character level
- using the wrong spellcasting ability for a spell
- assuming one class's focus works for every class's spells
- forgetting to round down when adding half-level or one-third-level spellcasting contributions
- assuming higher-level spell slots automatically grant spells of that circle

Keeping class-based spell knowledge separate from shared spell slots will prevent most confusion.

## Quick Reference

For multiclass spellcasting:

- determine spells known or prepared separately for each class
- each spell uses the spellcasting ability of the class it belongs to
- a spellcasting focus works only for the class associated with that focus
- combine spellcasting levels to determine your shared spell slots
- full casters add all levels
- paladin, ranger, and warlock add half levels, rounded down
- one-third casters add one-third of their levels, rounded down
- higher-level slots can cast lower-level spells, but do not grant access to higher-level spells by themselves
